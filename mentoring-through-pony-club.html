Faith, Service, and the Next Generation: Mentoring Through Pony Club

📅 January 20, 2026
✎ Celeste White
⌚ 5 min read

A riding lesson is rarely just a riding lesson. For Celeste White, it has been a two-decade masterclass in mentorship.

Among the many nonprofit boards and youth initiatives Celeste White has supported across Northern California, her work mentoring future leaders in the U.S. Pony Club stands out for how directly it mirrors her broader philosophy: real growth happens in person, over time, one relationship at a time.

What Pony Club Actually Teaches

On paper, the U.S. Pony Club teaches horsemanship. In practice, White has found it teaches something closer to character — patience with an animal that doesn't care about your schedule, responsibility for a creature that depends on you, and the humility of getting it wrong in front of other people.

Those are the same qualities she looks for in the scholars and writers Lux Forum brings into conversation with local communities — a connection she's explored further in her work founding Lux Forum.

"A horse won't pretend to respect you. Neither will a twelve-year-old. Both will tell you exactly where you stand."

Faith and Service, Side by Side

Pony Club is one piece of a much longer record of service. Celeste White has spent decades supporting organizations including The Salvation Army, Hospice, and Ag 4 Youth, work she traces back to her years as a graduate and now Trustee of Westmont College.

That consistency — showing up for the same causes, in the same region, across decades — is, in her view, what separates genuine community investment from a rotating list of good causes.

Mentorship as a Way of Life

It's a theme that follows Celeste home, too, to the St. Helena ranch she shares with her husband, Dr. Robert White, where the same outdoor, hands-on approach that shapes her Pony Club mentoring also shapes daily life.

Followers of her day-to-day life, including glimpses of the ranch itself, can find more through her Instagram page.

Conclusion

Mentorship, faith, and land turn out to be less separate than they first appear — a connection explored further on Celeste White's complete story.
